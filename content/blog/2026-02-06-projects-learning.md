A quick poll on my YouTube channel asked: What's the most effective way to learn programming? The clear winner? Building real projects—hands down.

With 15 years in software development, I couldn't agree more. The top choice aligns perfectly with what I've seen work time and again: jump in and create something useful for actual people, not just another to-do app from a tutorial.

Why does this approach stand out?

- **Real motivation and relevance** — When you're solving a genuine problem—like building an online shop for your aunt's small business or a simple tool to fix a pain point in someone's workflow—you stay engaged. The stakes feel higher, and the learning sticks because it's tied to something meaningful.
- **Embrace the struggle (it's where growth happens)** — Googling errors, debugging, and figuring things out on the fly is frustrating at first, but it's incredibly effective. This "struggling and searching" ranked second in the poll, and for good reason: it builds problem-solving muscle that passive watching never can.
- **Targeted learning beats endless theory** — Courses, tutorials, and docs are valuable, but don't overdo them upfront. Spend an hour or two on what you need right now (e.g., a React concept for your specific feature), then build. You'll retain far more when you apply it immediately. Once you've used a tool in a real context, diving deeper later—like optimizing performance—becomes easier and more purposeful.
- **Avoid over-discussing** — Pair programming or collaborating with other devs can be great, but it ranked lower. Sometimes teams get stuck in endless debates about "the best" architecture or performance tweaks instead of shipping. Solo real-world building forces you to decide, iterate, and move forward—skills that matter in the real world.

Even after all these years, every new project teaches me something fresh. Sure, I know React and backend basics inside out, but building forces me to tackle marketing, user needs, deployment quirks, and more. It's not just coding; it's creating value.

In 2026, with AI tools speeding up code generation, the fundamentals remain: hands-on building develops the intuition, resilience, and practical judgment no tutorial alone can provide. Start small, solve real problems, and iterate. That's how you truly level up.
